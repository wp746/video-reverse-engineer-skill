# Prototype Reconstruction Framework

Use this reference during **Stage 1 (Prototype Deconstruction)** and **Stage 2 (Style & Theme Migration)**. 

---

## 1. What is Prototype Reconstruction?

**Prototype Reconstruction (原型重构)** is a professional Hollywood-style creative methodology. Instead of copying a reference video literally, we deconstruct it into its underlying creative, physical, and sensory "prototype layers." 

By separating the "skeleton" (underlying structure, composition, camera motion, pacing) from the "skin" (specific characters, dialogue, setting, visual style), we can migrate this prototype into an entirely new thematic container or switch its aesthetic style (e.g., from animation to cinematic photorealism) with absolute precision.

---

## 2. The 10-Layer Deconstruction Standard

Every reference video must be analyzed across these 10 distinct layers. Do not combine or skip them.

### Layer 1: Plot Structure (逐剧情)
Identify the narrative beats, dramatic joints, and core turning points.
- Map the narrative into a standard structural curve (e.g., Setup -> Escalation -> Climax -> Twist -> Resolution).
- Define the dramatic goal of each scene.

### Layer 2: Character Archetypes (逐角色)
Extract the core character identities, interpersonal dynamics, and visual recognition anchors.
- Map characters to narrative functions (e.g., Protagonist, Catalyst, Antagonist).
- List stable physical traits (age feel, expression pattern, clothing silhouette).

### Layer 3: Visual Style & Textures (逐风格)
Deconstruct the aesthetic style, color palette, lighting rules, and surface textures.
- Define color temperature, saturation levels, and grading styles.
- Identify the texture code (e.g., wet/rainy reflections, film grain, metallic glares).
- Separate the style's medium (e.g., 2D hand-drawn, 3D claymation, 35mm film photorealism).

### Layer 4: Prop Systems (逐道具)
Extract critical narrative objects that drive the plot or carry symbolic weight.
- Identify functional props (objects characters interact with physically).
- Identify symbolic props (objects representing underlying emotional beats).

### Layer 5: Verbal Strategy (逐台词/旁白)
Analyze the voiceover (VO) and dialogue rhythm, subtext, and thematic anchor lines.
- Define the relation between text and image (e.g., reinforcement, counterpoint, narrative guidance).
- Identify the key line that acts as the philosophical anchor of the piece.

### Layer 6: Composition & Framing (逐构图)
Deconstruct the spatial staging, focal length, depth-of-field, and framing principles.
- Identify recurring composition types (e.g., golden ratio, absolute symmetry, wide-angle low-horizon, extreme depth).
- Note the focal length feel (e.g., compressed telephoto look vs wide-angle spatial distortion).

### Layer 7: Camera Movement Profiles (逐运镜)
Analyze physical camera translation, speed curve, mechanical/handheld tendencies, and vibrations.
- Note exact physical movements: pans, tilts, dollies, crane shots, and zooms.
- Describe the movement's sensory weight (e.g., high-inertia steady mechanical glide vs micro-jitter handheld breathing).

### Layer 8: VFX Boundaries (逐特效)
Identify simulation, atmospheric, and elemental special effects.
- Map fluid dynamics (water splashes, smoke, fire), particle systems, and energy flow boundaries.
- Define how effects interact physically with spatial surfaces and materials.

### Layer 9: Sound & Ambience (逐音效)
Analyze acoustic design, ambient pressure, sync sound beats, and musical hooks.
- Identify the acoustic space (e.g., damp enclosed reverb, vast dry desert wind).
- Map how SFX syncs with physical actions (e.g., heavy metallic thuds, low-frequency atmospheric hums).

### Layer 10: Narrative Pacing (逐叙事节奏)
Deconstruct the chronological cutting rate, compression points, tension build-ups, and dramatic beats.
- Map shot duration changes (e.g., rapid cuts during actions, long contemplative holds during emotional pivots).
- Quantify the emotional compression ratio (tension pressure vs release).

---

## 3. Style & Theme Migration Rules

Once the 10 prototype layers are locked, you must handle translation.

### Style Switching Rules (风格转译)
When migrating from one style medium to another (e.g., **Animation to Cinematic Photorealism**):
1. **De-couple input textures**: Strip away the animation artifacts (line art, flat shading, digital paint fills).
2. **Re-couple realistic textures**: Substitute with physical-world equivalents.
   - *Skin*: Add visible pores, micro-sweat, subsurface scattering, and natural peach fuzz.
   - *Costumes*: Convert digital fills to heavy fabrics (e.g., distressed leather, organic coarse linen, wool tweed) with physical stitch seams.
   - *Lighting*: Switch from flat animation fills to cinematic three-point lighting (soft diffused key, sharp rim highlight, textured fill).
3. **Rebuild the Camera**: Replace stylized animation camera movements with physical camera limitations (realistic lens distortion, real mechanical slider speed, natural lens flares, authentic depth-of-field blur).

### Theme Migration Rules (主题迁移)
When migrating the core narrative structure into a new story shell (preserving the prototype's emotional force):
1. **Identify the Core Proposition**: Pinpoint the underlying message (e.g., "The pain of letting go of the past").
2. **Select the New Setting & Era**: Place the new version in a distinct setting (e.g., changing a cybernetic future to a retro-futuristic 1980s or an ancient dynasty).
3. **Map the Equivalents**:
   - Replace Character A (e.g., an old cyborg) with Character B (e.g., an aging clockmaker).
   - Replace Prop A (e.g., a glowing power core) with Prop B (e.g., a hand-wound pocket watch).
   - Keep the exact structural beats, emotional curves, and camera motions intact.
